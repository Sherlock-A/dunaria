The desert punishes the unprepared and forgives the over-prepared. The common mistakes: too-thin shoes that fill with sand, no jacket for a 4 °C desert night, or a camera battery that dies before sunrise. Here's the complete packing list, with specific notes for each season.

## Clothing

### Layers are everything
The desert swings between extremes — a 35 °C afternoon can become a 10 °C night in spring, and well below 0 °C in January. The key principle: **layers you can add or remove**, not single heavy items.

**Upper body**:
- 2 long-sleeve lightweight shirts (linen or fast-dry synthetics) — sun protection for the day, warmth at night
- 1 fleece or light down jacket — for evenings and nights (essential year-round except July–August)
- 1 thin T-shirt or base layer

**Lower body**:
- 1–2 pairs of light trousers (not jeans — too hot and take forever to dry)
- Shorts if you prefer for camp, but long trousers are better in the dunes

**Head and face**:
- A hat or cap with a brim — non-negotiable in summer
- A lightweight scarf or shemagh — useful for sand protection in wind and warmth at night
- Sunglasses with UV protection

**Socks and underwear**:
- Lightweight, quick-dry. Merino wool socks are worth it — they manage temperature better than cotton.

---

## Footwear

This is where most people make mistakes.

**For the dunes**: Sand gets into everything that isn't fully closed. Running shoes or light hiking shoes work well. Avoid sandals for the actual dune climbing — hot sand, ankle instability on steep slopes.

**For camp and meals**: Flip-flops or light sandals are fine once you're at the camp.

**What not to bring**: High heels (obvious), thin-soled shoes that won't insulate from hot sand, or stiff hiking boots that you haven't broken in.

---

## Sun and heat protection

- **Sunscreen SPF 50+**: Apply it before you get in the vehicle in the morning and top up before any outdoor activity. Desert UV is intense even in winter.
- **Lip balm with SPF**: Lips crack and burn faster than skin in dry desert air.
- **Insect repellent**: Not a major issue at Erg Chebbi, but useful near palmeries and oases.

---

## Water and food

Most camps and tours include meals and water. But for the driving days:

- Carry at least **1.5 litres of water per person** in the vehicle (more in summer)
- A small **snack supply** (nuts, dried fruit, energy bars) for the long stretches between towns
- A refillable water bottle to avoid buying plastic at every stop

---

## Electronics and photography

- **Camera battery chargers × 2** if you're a photographer — there may not be power for 12+ hours
- **Portable power bank** (10,000–20,000 mAh) for phone charging at camp
- **Dust-protective bag or case** for your camera — fine sand gets into everything
- **Memory cards**: bring more than you think you'll need; the dunes at sunrise produce many photos
- **Torch / headlamp** with spare batteries — the dune walks at 5am are in complete darkness

---

## Medications and first aid

- Your regular prescriptions in original packaging (Morocco customs)
- **Ibuprofen + paracetamol**: headaches are common in heat
- **Oral rehydration salts**: if you feel the early signs of heat exhaustion (dizziness, nausea)
- **Diarrhoea tablets**: stomach issues are possible when diet changes abruptly
- **Plasters/blister pads**: dune climbing in unfamiliar shoes

---

## Documents and money

- Passport (required for camp check-in; some camps copy it)
- Cash in Moroccan dirhams — ATMs are available in Merzouga village but unreliable; withdraw in Erfoud or Ouarzazate
- Credit card (useful in larger towns; not in camps)

---

## What to leave at home

- Heavy hiking boots (unnecessary on the main Merzouga dunes)
- Hair dryer and styling products (no power, no point)
- Lots of jewellery (sand and sweat damage it, theft risk)
- More than one book (space and weight; your phone has plenty)
- A full suitcase — a 30–40L backpack is ideal. Large rolling suitcases are impractical on sandy paths.

---

## Seasonal adjustments

**Spring/Autumn**: The standard list above covers everything. A light fleece for evenings is essential.

**Summer**: Replace the jacket with a lightweight UV-resistant shirt. Add oral rehydration salts to the first-aid kit. A small personal fan helps.

**Winter**: Add a proper warm jacket (down or synthetic), thermal base layers, a sleeping bag rated to -5 °C if you're sleeping in a tent, and wool hat + gloves.
