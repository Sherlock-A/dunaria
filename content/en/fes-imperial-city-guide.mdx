Fes is Morocco's most complex city and probably the most rewarding for those who take time to explore it. Unlike Marrakech, where the medina has evolved into a sophisticated tourist hub, Fes feels real: artisans work as they did six centuries ago, neighborhoods have their own life, and getting lost among its 9,000 alleys is not a metaphor — it's literally possible.

---

## The Fès el-Bali medina

The old city of Fes (Fès el-Bali) has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1981 and is one of the largest medieval medinas in the world. It has over 150,000 inhabitants, 9,000 alleys, and an urban logic dating back to the 9th century.

### The medina's neighborhoods

The medina isn't a uniform whole. It has zones with very different personalities:

- **Kairouyine** (around the mosque): the historic heart with Al-Attarine Medersa and Al-Qarawiyyin University (859 AD, the world's oldest continuously operating university).
- **Al-Andalus** (northern quarter): founded by Andalusian exiles in the 9th century. Quieter, less touristy.
- **Mellah** (Jewish quarter): to the south, well-preserved. Converted synagogues and characteristic covered-balcony architecture.
- **Rcif**: the most popular and bustling quarter, along the Boufekrane river.

## What to see in Fes

### Bou Inania Medersa
The only medersa (Quranic school) in Fes with an integrated mosque. Dating from the 14th century, the wall ornamentation (zellij, stucco and carved cedar wood) is the most refined in the city.

### Al-Attarine Medersa
Adjacent to the Kairouyine Mosque. One of the most perfect madrasas of medieval Islam.

### Chouara Tanneries
The leather tanning quarter, visible from the terraces of surrounding leather shops. In the circular pits, leather is dyed in the same colors with the same methods as in the 11th century. The smell is intense; shops offer mint sprigs at the entrance.

### Mellah and Ibn Danan Synagogue
The historic Jewish quarter. The 17th-century synagogue is the oldest preserved in Morocco, restored with help from the Moroccan Jewish community in France.

## Navigating the medina

The Fes medina is the only one of the four imperial cities where getting lost is almost guaranteed, even with Google Maps. Alleys have no uniform names, and many entrances look like private courtyards.

**Practical strategy:**
1. Always enter through one of the main gates (Bab Bou Jeloud, the "blue gate," is the standard entrance from a taxi).
2. For a first visit, hiring an official guide for the first 3 hours saves a lot of confusion and opens conversations you wouldn't have alone.
3. The medina has no cars inside — the only transport is on foot or by cargo donkey.

## Where to eat in Fes

- **Café Clock**: has become an institution. Live music, cheese briouats, camel burgers. Perfect local-tourist mix.
- **Medina restaurants** near Rcif: no English menu, real prices.
- **Around Bab Bou Jeloud**: terraces with views of the blue gate, touristy atmosphere but decent food.

---

## Where to stay

- **Medina (riads)**: the most authentic experience. Waking up to the call to prayer and breakfasting in 18th-century courtyards. Prices: 300-600 MAD/night.
- **Fès el-Jdid / new town**: quieter, more practical for getting around, less atmospheric.

---

## How much time to spend

- **1 day**: you can see the highlights but without depth.
- **2 days**: the minimum recommended. One day for the main medina (Kairouyine, tanneries, Bou Inania), another for the Mellah and Al-Andalus.
- **3 days**: ideal. Add a half-day for Meknès (1h by bus/taxi) and another for exploring neighborhoods outside the usual tourist circuit.
