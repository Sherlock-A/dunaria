Essaouira has many identities — blue city, wind city, UNESCO medina — but one sets it apart from every other city in Morocco: it's the **world capital of Gnawa music**. If you don't know Gnawa, this guide explains what it is, what it sounds like and where to experience it.

## What is Gnawa music?

**Gnawa** is a musical and spiritual tradition that arrived in Morocco with enslaved people from sub-Saharan Africa between the 16th and 19th centuries. The Gnawa enslaved came mainly from what is today Mali, Guinea and Senegal, bringing their rhythms, instruments and healing rituals with them.

Over time, Gnawa merged with Moroccan Islamic spirituality and Amazigh elements, creating a unique hybrid tradition.

### The instruments

Gnawa's sonic identity comes from three main instruments:

**The guembri (sintir)**: a three-string bass with a wooden body and camel-skin top. It produces a deep, hypnotic sound that is the backbone of Gnawa music. The lead musician (the *maalem*) plays the guembri and sings.

**The krakeb (qraqab)**: large metal castanets, pincer-shaped. Played by the musicians accompanying the maalem. The metallic rhythmic sound of the krakeb is unmistakable — it's the sound of Essaouira's street Gnawa.

**The tbel (drum)**: in some ritual contexts a large drum marks the base rhythm.

### The lila: the complete ritual

**Gnawa** is not just music — it's also a healing ritual called *lila* (meaning "night" in Arabic). In a lila, the maalem guides an all-night ceremony in which the *mluk* (spirits) associated with colours and elements are invoked. Certain spirits are believed to heal physical or mental illness.

A complete lila lasts all night and has coded parts: opening, colour sequences (each colour corresponds to a different spirit), and closing at dawn.

Full ritual lilas are private and communal — they're not tourist shows. But adapted versions for visitors do exist.

## Why Essaouira is the Gnawa capital

Essaouira was for centuries an important trading port. Many enslaved people arrived through this port, and many stayed in the city. The Gnawa community of Essaouira was already large in the 18th century, and the city became the centre of transmission of the tradition.

Today, the Gnawa of Essaouira are recognised as the most authentic custodians of the tradition. In **2019, UNESCO inscribed Gnawa as Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity**, in a candidacy where the Essaouira community played a central role.

## The Gnaoua and World Music Festival

The **Gnaoua and World Music Festival** is the most important musical event in Essaouira and one of the most significant in the Arab world. It's held every year in **June** (normally the last weekend) and has been running since 1998.

### How the festival works

- **Free concerts**: the main performances are in Plaza Moulay Hassan and on the beach — free for all audiences.
- **Paid concerts**: smaller performances in theatres and covered spaces with tickets.
- **Fusions**: the festival formula puts Gnawa in dialogue with other genres: jazz, blues, reggae, electronic music. Concerts feature a Gnawa maalem + New Orleans jazz musician, or a flamenco singer + guembri.
- **Workshops and exhibitions**: during the day there are krakeb learning sessions, photography exhibitions and talks.

### Attendance and practical tips

The festival attracts 400,000-500,000 people over a weekend. Essaouira has limited hotel capacity — if you want to sleep in the city during the festival, **booking 3-4 months ahead is essential**.

The alternative is to base yourself in Marrakech and make the day trip, or book in riads in nearby villages.

Entry to plaza concerts is free — arrive 30-60 minutes before main concerts.

## Where to hear Gnawa outside the festival

Gnawa is present in Essaouira all year round, not just at the festival.

**Plaza Moulay Hassan**: Gnawa musicians play in the square almost daily, especially in evenings and at night. The atmosphere is informal — it's street music, not a show. Tip if you listen.

**Zaouia Sidna Bilal**: the Gnawa brotherhood of Essaouira has its headquarters here. On special occasions there are semi-public ceremonies. Ask locally for information.

**Live music restaurants**: some medina restaurants programme live Gnawa performances at night. Ask at your riad.

## Learning Gnawa

There are krakeb and guembri workshops in Essaouira, organised by local associations. Some riads and guesthouses run them for their guests. A 2-hour introductory workshop typically costs 200-300 MAD per person.

The guembri requires years of practice to master — the maalem learns from their teacher since childhood — but playing krakeb in basic rhythm can be learned in one session.
