Jemaa el Fna is not a monument — it's an ongoing performance. The square measures barely 300 metres across, but within it coexist (at different hours) orange juice vendors, snake charmers, acrobats, Arabic oral storytellers, Gnawa musicians and one of the world's most photographed open-air food markets. It's chaotic, noisy and impossible to ignore.

The key to enjoying it: understand that it has two completely distinct lives — daytime and night-time.

---

## Morning (7am-1pm): the square in quiet mode

**7-9am**: The square is almost empty. The first orange juice stalls arrive in a row. This is the only time of day when Jemaa el Fna has enough perspective to appreciate its real size. The Koutoubia in the background, the first light — it's the different photograph that most visitors never get because they sleep past 9.

**9am-1pm**: Activity builds. Acrobats and street performers set up their spaces. Spice stalls and the water sellers in traditional dress (the Guerrab) are in position. The first groups of snake charmers and photographers with falcons appear — with their own unannounced photo fees.

**Practical note on photos**: Men with snakes and monkeys at Jemaa el Fna deliberately position themselves in front of your camera. If the animal enters your shot, they'll ask for money. If you don't want to pay, don't photograph in their direction.

---

## Midday (1-3:30pm)

Daytime activity drops. This is when the square's perimeter cafés-restaurants come into their own: the Café de France is the best known; there are less touristy alternatives in the side alleys.

The food stalls haven't set up yet — that's the evening event.

---

## Afternoon (3:30-7pm): the transformation

From 3:30pm, the assembly begins. The square's food stalls take position: 100 numbered stalls, each with a tout who comes out to the street to attract diners ("Best tagine!", "Fresh seafood!"). The set-up process itself is photogenic.

By 5pm the square is full. Gnawa musicians (with their guembris and qraqeb) have fixed positions on the north side. Amazigh musicians with lutes and drums have theirs to the south. The Halqa oral storytellers (whose tradition is what UNESCO considers Intangible Cultural Heritage) tell stories in Moroccan Arabic with a circle of listeners around them.

---

## Night (7-11pm): the heart of the event

Jemaa el Fna's night food market is one of the world's most democratic food events: from 20 MAD for a bowl of harira to 200 MAD for a seafood platter, taking in brochettes, tagines, spiced snail broth (babouche) and dates. Quality varies but the experience has no equivalent.

**How to choose a stall**:
- Low-numbered stalls (1-20) are the most established and have more tourists
- High-numbered stalls (50+) have more locals and lower prices
- The mediocre stall trick: the tout inflates the price at the end. Ask for the price before sitting down, or simply avoid the stall

**What to eat**: Harira (Morocco's tomato, chickpea and lentil soup), kefta brochette, chicken tagine with olives, snails in spiced broth (more interesting than they sound).

---

## Viewpoints

**Café de France** (on the square's edge): The most photographed. Direct view from a table. Tourist prices (25-30 MAD for a coffee) but the view justifies it.

**Café-riad Bougainvillea** (5 min walk, inside the medina): Private terrace of a riad-café. Partial view of the square and medina rooftops. Less known, equally interesting.

**Ben Youssef Madrasa terrace** (10 min walk): Technically not a square viewpoint, but has a medina rooftop view that complements the square experience well.

---

## The Halqa: the art of oral storytelling

The Halqa oral storytellers are the official reason UNESCO declared Jemaa el Fna Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2001. They're storytellers — a blend of medieval oral tradition and contemporary daily life, with an audience that participates, laughs and debates.

The challenge for Western visitors: the narration is in Moroccan Arabic dialect (darija), not standard Arabic. But the physical language — the storyteller's rhythm, the audience's reactions — has value even when you don't understand the words.

---

## Getting there

Jemaa el Fna is in the medina's centre. From the airport: taxi (≈85 MAD) or bus no. 19 (1h, 4 MAD). From the Gueliz (new city): calèche (≈50 MAD) or taxi (≈20-25 MAD).
