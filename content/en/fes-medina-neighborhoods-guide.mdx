The Fes medina is the most alive, most complex and most confusing of all North African medinas. With 9,000 alleys, 150,000 residents and 12 centuries of history layered without clear signage, entering unprepared is an intense experience. This guide helps you navigate it.

---

## The five main neighborhoods

### Fès el-Bali (the old medina)
The historic heart and what people imagine when they think of the Fes medina. Around the Kairouyine Mosque (the world's oldest university, 9th century), all traditional commercial and religious life is organized.

**What to look for:** Bou Inania Medersa, Al-Attarine Medersa, Chouara tanneries, spice and craft souks.

**Atmosphere:** Touristy on the main axis (from Bab Bou Jeloud to Kairouyine), more authentic on side streets.

### Al-Andalus Quarter
The least visited of the historic neighborhoods. Founded in the 9th century by exiles from Al-Andalus (Muslim Spain), it has a namesake mosque and a much quieter pace than the rest.

**What to look for:** Al-Andalus mosque, the Rcif market, neighborhood life without tourists.

### The Mellah
The historic Jewish quarter of Fes, founded in the 13th century. Distinguished by its covered wooden balconies (typical of Judeo-Moroccan architecture) and multi-story houses. Ibn Danan Synagogue (17th century) is restored and open to the public.

**What to look for:** Balcony architecture, Jewish cemetery, covered market (fonduk).

### Fès el-Jdid (the new medina)
Built in the 13th century, it's the "new" medina in historical terms but has nothing modern about it. It houses the Royal Palace (impressive exterior, closed to the public) and the fountains quarter (Moulay Abdallah).

**What to look for:** The golden gates of the Royal Palace, Jnan Sbil garden.

### Tanneries area
Straddling Fès el-Bali and the leather quarter, this zone concentrates the most photogenic craft activity: tanneries, weavers, blacksmiths.

## The least saturated routes

80% of tourists enter through Bab Bou Jeloud, walk to the tanneries and leave through the same gate. Less crowded alternatives:

**Northern route:** Enter through Bab Guissa, walk toward the Bab Mahrouk cemetery with panoramic medina views, descend through the Al-Andalus quarter. Very few tourists.

**Mellah route:** Start at the Mellah (near the Royal Palace), climb toward Kairouyine from the less-frequented side, exit through Rcif.

**Early morning:** The tanneries are most photogenic first thing (light and fewer tourists). Arrive before 9am.

---

## Common mistakes

- **Following spontaneous "guides"** who offer their services at the blue gate: many lead to commission shops, not to the monuments you want. Official guides carry a Ministry of Tourism badge.
- **Entering without water**: hot days in the medina are exhausting. There are cold water stalls but bringing a bottle avoids confusion.
- **Expecting consistent WiFi or 4G**: coverage inside the medina is erratic. Download offline maps (Maps.me) before entering.
- **Booking accommodation outside the medina**: taxis can't enter. The best riads are reached on foot through alleys — practice the directions beforehand.

---

## Best time to visit

The medina is best early morning (8-11am) and at dusk (5-8pm). Midday in summer is brutally hot and artisans close for rest.
