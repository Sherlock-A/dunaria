The Moroccan desert circuit is one of the most photogenic routes in the world. Every hour of the drive south from Marrakech produces different light, texture, and colour. But knowing which spots to prioritise, when to arrive, and what settings to use makes the difference between holiday snapshots and photos worth printing. Here's the practical guide.

## The golden rule

**The best light on the route happens twice a day**: the hour after sunrise and the hour before sunset. Everything in between is harsher, flatter, and less interesting — useful for documentary shots but not for the images you came for.

Plan your driving days to hit the most important spots during these windows. This sometimes means driving in the dark to arrive before dawn — worth it.

---

## Spot 1: Erg Chebbi dunes at sunrise

**Why**: The most iconic photography location on the route. The dunes of Erg Chebbi at sunrise go through a rapid sequence of light: grey-blue (20 min before sun), warm gold (first 15 min of sun), deep orange-amber (next 30 min). Each phase is a different photo.

**How to get there**: Walk or ride camel from your camp to a high dune 20–30 minutes before sunrise. The timing is everything — arrive on the dune before the sun breaks the horizon.

**Composition approaches**:
- **Wide + silhouette**: A person or camel silhouetted against the orange sky, with the dune ridgeline as the horizon line
- **Shadow patterns**: Early morning low sun creates long, graphic shadows on the dune faces — best with a wide lens from a high point looking across the dune field
- **Sand texture**: Get close to the ground with a low angle, focus on the ripple patterns in the sand

**Technical**: On a phone — portrait mode off, tap to focus on the shadow areas rather than the bright sky. On a camera — spot meter on the sky, let foreground go dark, recover shadows in post. ISO 200–400 at sunrise; a tripod is only needed in the pre-dawn dark.

## Spot 2: Aït Ben Haddou

**Why**: The most architecturally compelling stop on the circuit. The earthen kasbahs photograph beautifully in low light, and the valley setting allows for wide establishing shots.

**Best light**: Morning (9–10am) when the sun hits the west face of the ksar directly, or evening (4–6pm) when the warm light angles across the towers.

**Best angles**:
- **Classic far-bank shot**: Cross the river, walk 200m downstream, shoot back toward the ksar with the palmerie in background. Works at any time of day but is best with late afternoon sun on the face.
- **Narrow alleyway inside the ksar**: Low light, textured walls, local life — classic travel photography. Best at midday when diffused light bounces off the walls.
- **High tower view**: Climb to the upper kasbahs for a looking-down shot over the village rooftops toward the valley.

**Technical**: In the alleys — wide lens, f/5.6–8 to keep walls sharp, bump ISO to 800–1600 if needed. The earthen tone of the walls is the color palette; don't over-saturate in post.

## Spot 3: Dadès Gorge — Monkey Fingers rock formation

**Why**: The eroded rock fingers are bizarre, otherworldly, and unlike anything else on the route. The colour contrast — deep red-ochre rock against pale sky — is naturally strong.

**Best light**: Late afternoon, when the sun is low and the rock faces glow.

**Approach**: Walk around the base of the formations, not just shoot from the road. Some scrambling puts you at mid-height for a level composition rather than looking up.

**Technical**: A polarising filter cuts the glare on the rock surfaces and saturates the colour significantly — the most useful filter on this entire route.

## Spot 4: Todra Gorge — the slot canyon

**Why**: 300-metre walls, 10 metres apart. The light at the gorge floor creates a narrow, deep blue strip of sky above. There's a brief window (around 11am–1pm, depending on time of year) when the sun shines directly into the gorge floor and creates an extreme contrast situation — either over-exposed sky or dark foreground.

**Best approach for photography**: Go earlier (9–10am) or later (3–4pm) when reflected light from the walls illuminates the floor softly. The less-photographed downstream section (walk 500m past the main tourist cluster) has better compositions.

**Technical**: Wide angle to capture the wall height. HDR or exposure bracketing if you're shooting the contrast window.

---

## Spot 5: Night sky

**Why**: Erg Chebbi sits in one of the lowest light-pollution areas in North Africa. On a clear, moonless night, the Milky Way core is plainly visible to the naked eye. The dune silhouettes provide natural foreground interest.

**When**: New moon periods in October and November give the darkest, clearest skies. Avoid the week around full moon.

**Technical (phone)**: Night mode with tripod. Frame the Milky Way with a dune or tent silhouette in the lower third. 15–30 second exposure depending on the mode. Don't use flash.

**Technical (camera)**: Wide lens (f/1.8–2.8 if possible), ISO 3200–6400, 20–30 second exposure (longer and stars begin to trail). Manual focus: set to infinity, confirm by zooming into a bright star in live view.

## Colour palette of the route

The Morocco desert circuit has its own visual language:

- **Sand**: Deep orange (Erg Chebbi), golden-brown (Erg Chigaga), tan (hamada plains)
- **Rock**: Ochre, rust, chocolate brown (Dadès), grey (Atlas)
- **Sky**: Intense blue (desert day), spectacular purple-orange (sunrise/sunset)
- **Architecture**: Earthy pink-ochre (pisé of Aït Ben Haddou), white-blue (Ouarzazate)

Work with this palette. Add a blue sky filter, saturate the earthy reds slightly, and protect the shadow detail in the earthen walls.

## Camera gear to bring

**Essential**:
- A dustproof bag for your camera — fine sand gets into every gap
- Extra batteries (2–3 fully charged before arriving; power is scarce)
- Extra memory cards (you'll shoot more than you expect)
- Polarising filter if you shoot with filters

**Nice to have**:
- A compact tripod for sunrise and night shots
- A remote shutter release for night sky

**Smartphone**: Modern phone cameras (iPhone 15 Pro, Pixel 8) handle this route impressively well. The native Night mode is excellent for stars. The main limitation is no optical zoom range for tight shots of camel caravans from a distance.
