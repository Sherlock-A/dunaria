Marrakech packs an unusual density of monuments, squares and characterful spaces into a small area. Most of the essentials fit into 2 days on foot. This list orders the must-sees by genuine importance rather than Instagram popularity.

## 1. Jemaa el Fna — the liveliest square in Africa

There's no bad time to visit, but two moments stand out: early morning (7-9am), when the square is nearly empty and the first juice vendors arrive, and at dusk (5-8pm), when it transforms completely with hundreds of food stalls, musicians and acrobats.

UNESCO's recognition as Intangible Cultural Heritage (2001) honours not the buildings but the living culture: the Halqa, the art of oral storytellers.

**Practical note**: Avoid vendors with photo animals (birds of prey, monkeys) — they insist on high payments afterwards.

---

## 2. The souks — the artisan labyrinth

The souks form a network of alleys north of Jemaa el Fna. Each area has its speciality:

| Souk | Speciality |
|---|---|
| Souk Semmarine | Main entrance, textiles, general crafts |
| Souk des Teinturiers | Dyers — the most photographed souk image |
| Souk Cherratin | Leather and leather goods |
| Rahba Kedima | Spices, medicinal plants, babouches |
| Souk Haddadine | Blacksmiths and metalworkers |

**Opening hours**: Active 9am-1pm and 3-7pm. Friday morning has the least activity (prayer day).

---

## 3. Ben Youssef Madrasa — Islamic architecture at its finest

The largest ancient Quranic school in the Maghreb, founded in the 14th century. The interior combines three decorative techniques that define Nasrid and Marinid architecture: zellij (tile mosaic), carved plaster (geometric arabesques) and worked cedar wood.

**Hours**: 9am-6pm. Entrance: 70 MAD (≈ £5). Allow at least 45 minutes — most visitors give it far less.

---

## 4. Bahia Palace — 19th-century luxury

Built between 1894 and 1900 for Si Moussa and his son Ahmed Ben Musa, intended to be the most beautiful palace in Morocco. The central courtyard with orange trees and the decorated reception rooms rank among the city's most photogenic interiors.

**Practical note**: Tour groups rush through in 20 minutes. With more time, the private apartment section (second floor, less visited) has notable mural paintings and painted polychrome cedar ceilings.

---

## 5. Majorelle Gardens and Yves Saint Laurent Museum

The garden created by Jacques Majorelle in the 1920s-30s, bought by Yves Saint Laurent and Pierre Bergé in 1980. The famous "Majorelle blue" coating the villa is a cobalt-cerulean shade registered as a colour by the painter.

The Yves Saint Laurent Museum (next door, separate entrance) has a permanent collection of haute couture and the designer's archive, including his Moroccan-inspired 1970s collections.

**Hours**: Garden 8am-6pm (5:30pm in winter). Garden entrance: 150 MAD. Museum: 100 MAD extra.
**Queue**: Up to 45 minutes in high season (July, August, Easter). Book online 2-3 days ahead.

---

## 6. The Koutoubia — the minaret that shaped Islamic architecture

The tallest and most imitated minaret in the Maghreb: 70 metres, built between 1147 and 1199 under the Almohad dynasty. Its proportions (1:5 width-to-height ratio) and geometric decoration were the direct models for Seville's Giralda and Rabat's Hassan Tower.

You can't go inside, but the green esplanade surrounding it has good angles for photography. Best shots: from the western side of the esplanade with the afternoon sun.

---

## 7. Saadian Tombs

16th-century mausoleums rediscovered in 1917 (sealed by Sultan Moulay Ismail two centuries earlier). The Hall of Twelve Columns, where the Saadian sultans are buried, has one of Morocco's most detailed muqarnas domes.

**Hours**: 9am-5pm. Entrance: 70 MAD. The space is small — expect a wait to enter in high season.

---

## 8. The Mellah — the historic Jewish quarter

The old Jewish quarter, adjacent to Bahia Palace. Unlike many other Moroccan medinas, Marrakech's mellah has been little transformed into museums or tourist shops: daily life continues. The Lazama synagogue, still in use, can be visited respectfully with a donation.

---

## 9. El Badi Palace — ruins of the world's richest palace

Built by Ahmed el Mansur in the 16th century with materials imported from Italy (onyx and marble) and from Sudan (gold). Demolished in 1696 by Moulay Ismail, who used the materials for his own palaces in Meknes. What remains are the walls and courtyard (135×135m) — the scale still impresses.

---

## 10. The Gueliz district and Moroccan Art Deco

The Gueliz, built by the French during the Protectorate (1912-1956), has a completely different architectural character from the medina: wide boulevards, Art Deco buildings with Islamic influences. Mohammed V Avenue has some of the best examples.

Combining an afternoon in the Gueliz with the Café du Livre (bookshop-café with an excellent selection of books about Morocco) gives a complete picture of the city.

---

## 11. Marrakech Museum

Housed in Mnebhi Palace, one of the medina's best-preserved palaces. The collection includes ceramics, metalwork, textiles and contemporary Moroccan art. The central courtyard (with an enormous hanging lamp) is itself one of the medina's most beautiful spaces.

---

## 12. The walls and gates — sunset walk

The 12th-century adobe walls encircle the medina for 19 km. The calèche ride along the outside of the walls at sunset — especially the stretch between Bab Doukkala and Bab el Khemis — is one of Marrakech's most peaceful and underrated activities.
