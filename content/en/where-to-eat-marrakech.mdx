Moroccan food has real depth: it's not just tagines and couscous (though both, well-made, are remarkable). Marrakech has tourist-facing restaurants alongside a local scene with decades of tradition. The difference between eating well and eating poorly here depends almost entirely on where you choose to sit.

---

## The basic rules

**Rule 1**: Restaurants with an English menu and laminated photo menus, located within the first 20 metres around Jemaa el Fna, are invariably more expensive and worse than those 5 minutes' walk into the medina interior.

**Rule 2**: A restaurant where the majority of diners are locals generally offers better value than one where everyone is a tourist.

**Rule 3**: A Moroccan breakfast (atay, msmen, khobz with amlou and honey) at a neighbourhood bakery or café costs 20-30 MAD and is better than the "continental breakfast" at many riads for 80 MAD.

## What to eat

### Tagine
Morocco's most iconic dish. A slow stew cooked in the conical clay pot of the same name. The most interesting variations:

- **Lamb tagine with prunes and almonds**: The classic sweet-savoury
- **Chicken tagine with olives and preserved lemon**: Balanced, acidic, done very well in Marrakech
- **Vegetable tagine**: Local vegetarian versions are surprisingly good

A well-executed tagine needs several hours of cooking. Those served in 10 minutes (many in the tourist zone) are reheated or rushed.

### Friday couscous
In Morocco, couscous is eaten on Fridays — the prayer day, in family settings. Restaurants that serve couscous the rest of the week are generally reheating the previous day's batch. For authentic couscous, go on a Friday (local restaurants have it fresh) or try it at a riad that makes it to order.

### Harira
Morocco's national soup: tomatoes, chickpeas, lentils, coriander, spices. Thick, nourishing, cheap. During Ramadan it's the fast-breaking meal; the rest of the year you find it in cafés at all hours. Price: 10-20 MAD.

### Pastilla (bastilla)
Morocco's most elaborate celebration dish: filo pastry (warka) filled with pigeon or chicken with almonds, egg and spices, dusted with icing sugar and cinnamon. The sweet-savoury combination seems strange the first time — it's extraordinary in practice. Found at formal restaurants, not street stalls.

### Mechoui
Slow-roasted whole lamb in a clay oven. There's a dedicated market in the medina (Mechoui Alley, next to Souk el Kebir) where specialist cooks sell portions directly from the oven. Price: 60-100 MAD for a generous portion.

### Jemaa el Fna street food
The square's night stalls are genuinely good — not just photogenic. The most interesting:

- **Snails in broth** (babouche): Simmered in an intense spice broth. Eaten with a small toothpick. A bowl: 10-15 MAD.
- **Kefta brochettes** (spiced minced meat): Universally good at local stalls.
- **Fresh dates with milk**: From the ambulant vendors. 10 MAD.

## Where to eat well

### Inside the medina: Derb Djedid and Kennaria

Derb Djedid street (behind Souk Cherratin) and the Kennaria streets have the highest concentration of restaurants catering to locals at reasonable prices. Look for the ones with a daily menu written on an exterior chalkboard, plastic tables and prices in MAD without decimals.

Full set menu (soup, main, dessert or tea) at this type of local: 50-80 MAD.

### Riad café-restaurants

Several riads have restaurants or dinner service open to non-guests. Dinner at an authentic riad — lit courtyard, elaborate traditional cooking — is in the range of 200-350 MAD per person with a drink. It's a different experience from street stalls, not better or worse: complementary.

### Gueliz for brunch or Western-style lunch

The modern district (Gueliz) has cafés and restaurants with more international menus: good for a break from tagine or for a relaxed brunch. The Mohammed V Avenue area has varied options.

## Drinks

**Atay** (mint tea): Morocco's tea par excellence, green tea with fresh mint and sugar. Poured from a height to aerate and create froth. Free or included in many lunches; 10-20 MAD in cafés. There's no reason to drink anything else in the medina.

**Jemaa el Fna orange juice**: The fresh-squeezed juice stalls lined up in a row are one of the square's symbols. Price: 5-10 MAD per glass. Wait for them to press it in front of you.

**Water**: Marrakech tap water isn't recommended for those not accustomed to it. Bottled mineral water: 5 MAD per litre in shops.

**Alcohol**: Available at hotels, licensed restaurants and some supermarkets (Carrefour in the Gueliz). The medina has no bars or alcohol sales.

| Style | Daily spend |
|---|---|
| Budget (harira, neighbourhood tagine, tea) | 80-150 MAD (£6-10) |
| Mid-range (local restaurant, evening at the square) | 150-300 MAD (£10-20) |
