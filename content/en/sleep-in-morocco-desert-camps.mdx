A night in the Sahara desert is one of those travel experiences that almost always exceeds expectations — even in a basic camp. The real sky away from light pollution, the complete silence, the temperature drop at sunset, and waking up to a sunrise that turns the dunes gold: it genuinely delivers. But the quality gap between a €15 camp and a €120 camp is enormous. Here's how to choose.

## The three tiers

### Tier 1: Basic Berber camp (€15–30/person)

The original experience, unchanged for decades. A cluster of traditional black caidal tents or basic Berber haimas, communal squat toilets, limited electricity (if any), and a simple dinner of tagine and bread cooked over a fire.

**What you get**:
- Shared sleeping area with mattresses and blankets
- Basic dinner and breakfast included
- Shared toilet facilities (usually functional but basic)
- Camel ride to/from the camp (usually included or offered at cost)

**Who it's for**: Budget travellers, those who want the most authentic local feel, and people who genuinely don't care about bathrooms.

**What to watch out for**: Some "basic camps" are located right on the edge of the village — literally in the sand but within sight of houses. The better ones are genuinely in the dunes, 20–40 minutes' walk or camel ride from the road. Ask specifically about location when booking.

---

### Tier 2: Mid-range camp (€40–80/person)

The sweet spot for most travellers. These camps combine proper private tents (with real beds, not just floor mattresses) with decent en-suite or shared bathrooms, better food, and some design care.

**What you get**:
- Private haima or furnished tent with a real bed and proper bedding
- Shared or en-suite bathroom with running water (cold in most, hot in better ones)
- A proper dinner — usually multiple courses including salads, tagine, couscous, mint tea
- Sitting area around a fire in the evening, often with Gnawa music
- Decent location in the dunes

**Who it's for**: Couples, groups of friends, families with kids old enough to enjoy the experience. Anyone who wants a real sleep without roughing it.

**Why this tier often wins**: The experience feels authentic while the basics work. At a good mid-range camp in October, you'll eat well, sleep comfortably, and wake up to an unforgettable sunrise — for about the cost of a reasonable hotel night.

---

### Tier 3: Luxury glamping (€90–200+/person)

The high end has expanded significantly in the last few years. Luxury desert camps now offer private en-suite bathrooms with hot showers, proper beds with quality linen, heated tents in winter, and sometimes a small private terrace or pool.

**What you get**:
- Large private tent with hotel-standard bed, power outlets, and sometimes AC
- Private en-suite bathroom with hot shower
- Multi-course dinner with table service
- Optional private camel ride or 4x4 excursion
- Some camps have a rooftop terrace for better stargazing

**Who it's for**: Honeymoons, special occasions, travellers who won't compromise on comfort, and photography enthusiasts who need reliable power for equipment.

**What luxury doesn't change**: The stars are exactly the same. The dunes look the same at sunrise. The wind sounds identical. What changes is whether you're comfortable enough to enjoy it fully.

## Key questions to ask before booking

**1. How far are you from the road?**
The magic disappears if you can hear cars or see village lights. Good camps are at least 20–30 minutes' walk (or 15 minutes by camel) from the road. Ask specifically.

**2. Is the camel ride included?**
Many camps include a short camel trek at sunset or sunrise. Others charge separately (€15–25/person). Clarify upfront.

**3. What's the toilet situation?**
Even at mid-range camps, "en-suite" sometimes means a private toilet shed 10 metres from your tent rather than attached to it. Not a dealbreaker but worth knowing.

**4. What do you actually get for the price?**
A quoted price of €60/person might mean dinner + breakfast + camel ride included, or just accommodation. Get the full list of inclusions.

**5. When was it last reviewed?**
Camp quality fluctuates. Read reviews from the last 6 months, not the five-star review from 2022.

## Practical notes

**Electricity**: Basic camps have limited or no power. Mid-range and luxury camps typically have power strips in tents for charging. A power bank is essential regardless of camp level — phone battery is often critical for sunrise alarm.

**Temperature**: Always bring a warm layer and ask the camp about blanket supply. Even in April, desert nights can be cooler than expected, and summer nights get cold after 2am.

**Connectivity**: No Wi-Fi at most camps. Mobile signal from Maroc Telecom or Orange works intermittently at Merzouga area camps; it disappears entirely in the deeper dunes.

**Booking**: In April, October, and around European school holidays, the best mid-range and luxury camps fill up 3–4 weeks in advance. Basic camps are usually available with less notice.
