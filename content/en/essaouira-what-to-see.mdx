Essaouira is a small city — you can walk the entire medina in under an hour — but it's full of spots with their own character. This list covers the ten must-sees, with the lesser-known ones at the end for those wanting to go beyond the usual circuit.

## 1. Skala de la Ville

The **north ramparts** with their bronze cannons overlooking the Atlantic are Essaouira's most iconic image. The view over the ocean from here is exceptional: seagulls gliding, waves breaking against the rocks, and the deep blue of the sea below.

Access via Rue Skala, on the north side of the medina. Free, always open. Go early in the morning to be alone — the ramparts fill with photographers at sunset.

## 2. The port and fish auction

The **blue port** of Essaouira is one of the most photogenic in Morocco. Blue and white fishing boats, nets spread on the dock, pelicans waiting for scraps.

If you arrive between 7am and 11am, you'll see the **criée** (fish auction): fishermen, middlemen and local cooks negotiating the day's catch. Authentic and noisy. Right next to it, stalls cook what was just pulled from the water.

## 3. The walled medina

Essaouira's **medina** has something different from Marrakech's or Fès's: it was designed by a European architect, which gives it wider streets and a more regular grid. Less labyrinthine, easier to navigate, and with that curious contrast between Moroccan architecture and almost French urban planning.

The main streets (Avenue de l'Istiqlal, Rue Mohamed el Qory) are the commercial axis. But the best corners are in the secondary streets to the north and east, where craftsmen's workshops are still operating.

## 4. The thuya wood souk

Essaouira has the world monopoly on **thuya** work, an aromatic wood with brown and yellow grain that grows only in this region. In the wood souk (Rue Attarine, north of the medina) there are workshops where craftsmen work by hand: boxes, chess boards, frames, bowls.

A well-crafted thuya chess set costs between 200 and 600 MAD (18-55€). Small objects at 50-150 MAD make good souvenirs.

## 5. Plaza Moulay Hassan

The **main square** of the medina is Essaouira's meeting point. Terrace cafés around it, street musicians, Moroccan families strolling at sunset and tourists mixed in. It's not a tourist trap — it's a living square.

The best time is 5pm to 8pm, when the light drops and the square fills with atmosphere. **Taros Café** on the corner has the best terrace and fills up fast — arrive before 6pm.

## 6. The main beach

The **beach of Essaouira** starts just south of the medina and extends several kilometres. Fine ocre-gold sand, with the backdrop of the ramparts.

In summer the Atlantic trade wind can be uncomfortable for sunbathing or swimming. But early morning (before 11am, before the alisio picks up) the beach is perfect.

## 7. Skala du Port

Less visited than the north ramparts, the **Port Skala** offers a different perspective: views over the port, boats and lighthouse. Also historical cannons here. Access from outside the medina, next to the fishing port entrance. Small entry fee (~10 MAD).

## 8. The Mellah (historic Jewish quarter)

Essaouira's **Jewish quarter** is one of the oldest and best preserved in Morocco. In the 19th century, the Jewish community was the majority in Essaouira — the city was a major trading port where Sephardic Jews played a central role in trade with Europe.

Today some restored synagogues remain and the architecture is different: wooden balconies, facades with Stars of David. The quarter is in the northwest of the medina.

## 9. Sidi Mohammed Ben Abdallah Museum

Housed in a 19th-century palace, the **regional museum** has collections of crafts, musical instruments (especially Gnawa instruments), Amazigh jewellery and everyday historical objects. Small but gives good cultural context. Entry ~20 MAD. Closed Tuesdays.

## 10. Sunset from the ramparts

The sunset over the Atlantic from Essaouira is spectacular. The north ramparts are the best spot, but the beach also gives a different perspective. The light on the white medina walls turns golden and warm — the exact opposite of the cold blue of midday.

Arrive at Skala de la Ville 30-40 minutes before sunset. There are photographers there every day.
