The Moroccan desert is one of the most seasonal destinations in the world. The difference between going in the right month and the wrong one can define your entire experience — from a perfect camel ride at sunset to sitting in a tent at 44 °C, unable to move between 11am and 5pm. Here's the honest breakdown, month by month.

## The short answer

**Best time: spring (March–May) or autumn (October–November).**

Both seasons offer warm days without extreme heat and cool but manageable nights. These are the windows when every activity — camel treks, hiking in the dunes, quad biking, night photography — is comfortable.

---

## Spring (March–May)

Spring is peak season for a reason: the desert is at its most photogenic and the conditions are nearly ideal.

**March**: Temperatures start to warm up (22–30 °C during the day). Some windy days early in the month can kick up sand, but the light is beautiful. Crowds are moderate until the second half of the month.

**April**: The best single month of the year. Days hit 25–33 °C, nights drop to a comfortable 12–17 °C. The sky is sharp, the colors are vivid, and every activity is on the table. Book camps at least 2–3 weeks in advance — the best spots fill up.

**May**: Already warm at midday (30–38 °C), but still excellent in the mornings and evenings. Spanish bank holidays mid-month can pack out certain camps. Still a very good time to go.

### Pros
- Perfect daytime conditions for all activities
- Exceptional photography light (longer golden hours)
- Flamingos at Dayet Srji lake near Merzouga (April–May)

### Cons
- Highest prices of the year
- Quality camps book up weeks in advance

---

## Autumn (September–November)

Almost identical to spring in terms of climate, but with less competition for campsites — especially in November.

**September**: The transition month. Early September can still be brutal (35–40 °C at midday). From mid-September, conditions improve rapidly. If you go in September, target the second half.

**October**: The standout autumn month. Days at 25–32 °C, nights at 12–18 °C. Spanish and French national holidays create some busy weekends, but overall it's a great choice.

**November**: The most underrated month. Tourist numbers drop noticeably, prices fall 10–15% vs October, and the weather remains very pleasant (20–28 °C days). Nights start getting cold toward the end of the month — pack a proper jacket and a warm layer for sleeping.

### Pros
- Near-identical weather to spring with fewer crowds (especially November)
- Better prices than peak season
- Extremely clear skies for stargazing

### Cons
- Early September still feels like summer
- Shorter days mean less golden-hour light than spring

## Summer (June–August)

The desert in summer is extreme. Midday temperatures regularly exceed 40 °C; in July and August, 45–48 °C is not unusual. The sand itself gets hot enough to burn through thin-soled shoes.

It's not impossible to visit in summer, but you need to understand the constraints:

- All outdoor activities (camel, hiking, quad) only work before 9am and after 6pm.
- The middle of the day means staying in your camp or riad with shade and a fan.
- Heat exhaustion is a genuine risk if you push it — hydrate constantly, cover your head and body.

**When summer works**: If summer is your only option, target **early June** or **late August** — the least extreme windows. Avoid mid-July to mid-August if you can.

**Summer upside**: 20–30% cheaper than spring, almost empty. If you want a genuinely solitary desert experience and can handle the heat, it's a different kind of memorable.

---

## Winter (December–February)

Winter gets a worse reputation than it deserves.

**Daytime conditions are good**: 15–22 °C is perfectly comfortable for camel rides, dune walks, and excursions. The low winter sun creates warm, cinematic light — some photographers specifically come in January for this.

**Nights are cold**: Desert nights can drop below 0 °C in January and February. Sleeping in a traditional tent without heating is possible with the right gear (a good sleeping bag rated to −5 °C, and layers), but uncomfortable if you come unprepared. Luxury camps usually have some heating.

**December**: More popular than you'd expect — many Europeans target Morocco for the New Year. The week around Christmas and New Year sees higher prices and fuller camps.

**January–February**: The quietest months of the year. Very few tourists, low prices, pleasant days. Only for travellers who don't mind cold nights.

## What about Ramadan?

Ramadan shifts each year (in 2026 it falls roughly in February–March). Desert camps largely continue operating normally since they cater to non-Muslim tourists. The main practical issue is finding food during the day in small villages — bring provisions for the road.
