Two days in Marrakech is enough to see the essentials without rushing, with time for the slower rhythm of cafés, souks and riad courtyards. With less time, prioritise day one complete. With more time, a third day is ideal for a day trip to the Atlas or Essaouira.

This itinerary assumes you arrive the evening of day 0 (or the night before) and leave the afternoon of day 2.

---

## Before you start

**Accommodation**: Book inside the medina, preferably in the north (Mouassine or Bab Doukkala area) or centre (near Souk Semmarine). Walkable proximity to the souks and Jemaa el Fna is the main advantage.

**Clothing**: In the medina, covered shoulders and knees. Not legally required, but the visit is more comfortable.

**Cash**: Have Moroccan dirhams (MAD) for souks, cafés and taxis. Bank ATMs in the Gueliz are the most reliable.

---

## Day 1: The medina and souks

### Early morning (7-9am): The medina before the tourists

Set the alarm for before 7am and step out into the medina alleys. At this hour, the bread ovens (farran) are at full work, the smell of freshly baked khobz fills the alleys, and the first café-au-lait vendors are opening. Jemaa el Fna is silent and you can cross it without anyone trying to sell you anything.

This quiet half-hour is what most people who experience it remember best.

**Breakfast**: Find a neighbourhood bakery (tables without a menu, bread and oil on the table) — 20-30 MAD for the full spread with mint tea.

### Mid-morning (9:30am-12pm): The souks in full swing

The souks open fully from 9am. This is the time to explore them with real activity: dyers at work, blacksmiths hammering metal, cobblers sewing babouches. The light in the covered alleys at this hour is lateral and photogenic.

Suggested order:
1. Enter via Souk Semmarine (the main covered street)
2. Turn right toward the Dyers (Souk des Teinturiers)
3. Climb to a terrace to see the dye vats from above
4. Return toward Souk Cherratin (leather)
5. Finish at Rahba Kedima (spices)

Ben Youssef Madrasa is 5 minutes' walk from the north end of the souks — fit it in before lunch.

**Souk shopping**: Read the [souks shopping guide](/en/blog/marrakech-souks-shopping) before negotiating. The opening price is not the real price.

### Lunch (12:30-2pm)

The best chicken tagine with olives and preserved lemon in Marrakech is not at the Jemaa el Fna restaurants — it's at the medina restaurants with no anglicisms and no photo menus. Look in Derb Djedid or in the streets around Ben Youssef Madrasa.

Budget: 60-100 MAD per person with tea.

### Afternoon (3:30-6:30pm): Palaces and monuments in the south medina

The south medina concentrates the largest historical monuments. Optimal afternoon route:

1. **Bahia Palace** (entrance 70 MAD): Visit at afternoon opening to have the courtyard almost alone
2. **El Badi Palace** (10 min walk): Ruins of the 16th century's most ambitious palace. The courtyard's scale still impresses despite later demolition.
3. **Saadian Tombs** (entrance 70 MAD): The Hall of Twelve Columns — one of the peaks of Islamic decoration in Morocco

### Evening (6:30-10pm): Jemaa el Fna at maximum intensity

Head to Jemaa el Fna when the food stalls are setting up. This is the square's most photogenic moment: warm sunset light, smoke from brochettes, the Koutoubia in the background. Gnawa musicians have been playing for hours.

Climb to the Café de France terrace 30 minutes before sunset for the aerial perspective.

**Dinner at the square** (8-9pm): Choose a stall on the northwest side (higher numbers) for less pressure and better prices. See the [where to eat in Marrakech guide](/en/blog/where-to-eat-marrakech) for more options.

## Day 2: Gardens, the Gueliz and perspective

### Morning (9am-12pm): Majorelle and the YSL Museum

Arrive at Majorelle Gardens at opening (9am) to see the cobalt blue in the morning light without the afternoon queue. If you're interested in Yves Saint Laurent's history, the adjacent museum has a permanent collection explaining the designer's 30-year relationship with Marrakech.

Then the Gueliz district (new city): good brunch cafés, French colonial architecture from the 1920s-50s, a completely different scale from the medina.

### Afternoon (2-6pm): Unplanned exploration

The second day is for alleys without an objective: stepping into a half-open riad door that offers a visit, discovering the cedar carpentry workshop in the woodworkers' souk, taking tea on a riad-café terrace looking over the medina rooftops.

For those with more time:
- **Ménara Gardens** (3km west): Free, tranquil, with the green pavilion and reflecting pool
- **Kasbah district**: Less visited than the centre, quieter, close to the south walls

### Evening: Riad dinner

If you have the energy, dinner at a riad (open to non-guests) is Marrakech's most complete gastronomic experience. Lit courtyard, elaborate traditional cooking, unhurried service. Book 1-2 days in advance.

## If you're continuing to the desert

Marrakech is the natural starting point for the desert route. The [day trips from Marrakech](/en/blog/day-trips-from-marrakech) and the [complete Morocco desert guide](/en/blog/morocco-desert-guide) provide the context for planning the continuation.

Departure from Marrakech toward Merzouga takes 8-9 hours via the Tichka Pass (2,260m), Ouarzazate and Aït Ben Haddou.
