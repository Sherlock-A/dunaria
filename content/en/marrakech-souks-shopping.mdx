Marrakech's souks are among the world's most photogenic shopping environments and, simultaneously, among the most confusing for visitors who don't know how they work. This guide gets straight to the point: what's worth buying, how negotiation works in practice and which products to avoid.

---

## The basic rule: the opening price means nothing

In Marrakech's souks, the first price you're given is approximately double (sometimes triple) what the vendor is willing to accept. This isn't a scam — it's the standard negotiation system that has worked here for centuries.

Negotiating isn't aggressive or unpleasant if it's done lightly. The vendor won't be offended if you counter at half price. You shouldn't be offended if they start at triple.

**The standard process**:
1. Ask the price without showing too much interest
2. Counter-offer at 40-50% of the asking price
3. Meet somewhere around 60-70% with some back-and-forth
4. Thank them and walk away if the final price doesn't work — they may come down further

**What doesn't work**: Showing extreme enthusiasm before asking the price, or letting on that you've been looking specifically for this item.

## What to buy in the souks

### Spices — the best value purchase

Spices in Marrakech's souks are significantly cheaper than in Europe or North America, and the quality of saffron and cumin is generally superior. What to buy:

- **Saffron** (za'afaran): Moroccan saffron is real, good quality and reasonably priced. Distrust suspiciously low prices (likely adulterated with safflower). Normal price: 25-40 MAD per gram.
- **Ras el hanout**: Morocco's spice blend (up to 30 different ingredients). Each vendor has their own mix.
- **Cumin, smoked paprika, dried ginger**: Excellent quality at very low prices.
- **Rose water** (ma' l-ward): For cooking and cosmetics. Pure versions without alcohol are the good ones.

**Reference souk**: Rahba Kedima and the Souk Semmarine entrances.

### Argan — Marrakech's most imitated product

Pure argan oil (both culinary, toasted, and cosmetic, untoasted) is one of the best possible purchases in Marrakech. The problem is the market is saturated with blended or diluted products.

**How to identify the genuine article**:
- Women's cooperatives produce the authentic version (well-known cooperatives on the Agadir road)
- Genuine cosmetic argan: slightly golden-yellow colour, mild nutty smell, non-greasy to the touch
- Quality price guide: 80-120 MAD per 30ml cosmetic

**Where to buy**: Prefer shops with a registered cooperative (they display a certificate) over souk stalls.

### Ceramics and zellij

Marrakech ceramics (plates, bowls, tagines) are attractive and affordable. The problem: fragile and difficult to transport. Some shops do international shipping with good packaging.

Zellij (tile mosaic) can only be purchased as individual tiles (complete compositions are architectural features, not travel items).

### Leather goods: bags, babouches and belts

Moroccan leather (especially from Fez) is good quality. Babouches (heelless slippers) are among the most popular purchases: comfortable, lightweight and genuinely useful.

**Worth knowing**: Babouches from Souk Cherratin are generally better quality (more durable) than those from Jemaa el Fna stalls, which produce specifically for tourists with lower durability.

### Brass lamps and decorative objects

The perforated brass lamps that project geometric patterns when lit are among the most representative objects of Moroccan craftsmanship. Souk Haddadine has the workshops.

**Transport tip**: Floor lamps come apart; hanging lamps with chains are trickier. Verify the vendor can package properly if you buy something fragile or large.

### Textiles: djellabas, kaftans and Atlas wool

Moroccan fabrics (especially Atlas wool) are high quality. Prices in Souk Semmarine for authentic pieces (not tourist souvenirs) range from 200 to 1,500 MAD depending on quality and workmanship.

## What to avoid buying

**Generic "argan" products at square stalls**: Generally blended or fake.

**Carpets without knowing provenance**: Authentic Moroccan carpets (especially Atlas ones) are investments of 500-5,000 MAD. Without specific knowledge of what's authentic, it's not the right moment to buy.

**Items with "antique" claims**: In the souks, anything can be "antique". Without specific expertise, it's difficult to distinguish.

**Mass-produced souvenirs labelled "Maroc"**: Available throughout Souk Semmarine at prices that seem low but are simply Chinese imports with local branding.

## Shopping logistics

**Currency**: Moroccan Dirham (MAD) only. Many vendors accept euros but give change in MAD at an unfavourable rate. Better to have dirhams.

**Group negotiation**: If you're with others and one person starts negotiating, the others shouldn't show interest — it gives the vendor too much information about how much you want the item.

**International shipping**: For large items (carpets, ceramics), several vendors have experience shipping to Europe. Request an invoice and tracking details.
