Marrakech hits you immediately. The alleys of the medina, the noise of Jemaa el Fna square, the smell of spices in the souks, the architecture of riads with their silent courtyards — all of it lands with physical force on first contact. This guide covers the essentials so your trip goes well from the start.

## Why visit Marrakech

Marrakech is more than a tourist destination. It's the gateway to Morocco's most ancient layers, the natural starting point for the Sahara desert, the Atlas Mountains and the Atlantic coast. In practical terms:

- **Direct flights** from across Europe and the Americas at competitive prices
- **Dense city**: most of the essentials are walkable from the medina
- **Perfect scale**: 2-3 days for the city, then day trips or onward to the south
- **Serious food** — tagine, couscous, pastilla and harira at excellent value

The "red city" (named for the colour of its adobe walls) is chaotic, intense and completely authentic. Tourism exists but doesn't dominate; daily local life continues its rhythm around and within it.

## What to see in Marrakech

### Jemaa el Fna
The medina's main square, listed as UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. During the day: jugglers, snake charmers, herbalists, orange juice vendors. At dusk: activity multiplies, food stalls appear, Gnawa musicians set up. Visit both moments.

The best-known viewpoint is the terrace of the Café de France at the square's edge, but the more interesting views are from riad-café terraces inside the medina, with cleaner sightlines.

### The medina and its souks
Marrakech's medina is a deliberate labyrinth. Craftsmen are grouped by trade: dyers, silversmiths, cobblers, weavers. Getting lost isn't a risk — it's a strategy. The main alleys always lead back toward Jemaa el Fna, the Koutoubia or one of the gates.

Most-visited souks: Souk Semmarine (textiles and general crafts), Souk des Teinturiers (dyers — the most photogenic), Souk Cherratin (leather goods), Rahba Kedima (spices and medicinal plants).

### The Koutoubia
Marrakech's most iconic minaret, visible from almost anywhere in the city. Built in the 12th century, it served as the model for Seville's Giralda and Rabat's Hassan Tower. Non-Muslims can't enter, but seeing it up close is worth five minutes.

### Bahia Palace
The best-preserved example of 19th-century Moroccan palace architecture. Central courtyard with orange trees, rooms decorated with zellij (mosaic), carved plasterwork and painted cedar wood. Tour groups rush through; arrive at opening (9am) and you'll have the courtyard to yourself for a few minutes.

### Ben Youssef Madrasa
Once the largest Quranic school in the Maghreb, built in the 14th century and restored in the 16th. The architecture is exceptional: carved plaster, mosaics and cedar wood cover every centimetre of the ablutions hall and prayer room. Worth the entrance fee — one of the best investments in Marrakech.

### Majorelle Gardens and Yves Saint Laurent Museum
Created by painter Jacques Majorelle, later acquired by Yves Saint Laurent. The cobalt blue villa set in a tropical garden is one of Marrakech's most photographed colour combinations. Long queues in high season; arriving at opening (9am) solves the problem.

Ménara Gardens: quieter, with the green pavilion and reflecting pool mirroring the Atlas on clear days. Free entry, no crowds.

## How to get there

**By plane**: Marrakech-Menara Airport (RAK), 6 km southwest of the centre. Direct flights from London, Paris, Madrid, Amsterdam, Brussels and many other European cities. Flights from North America with one stop (usually via Paris, Madrid or Casablanca).

**From the airport to the centre**: Taxi (negotiate price before, 80-100 MAD = £7-10, 20-30 min journey). Bus no. 19 (4 MAD, 1 hour, terminates at Jemaa el Fna).

**From other Moroccan cities**: ONCF train from Casablanca (3h), Rabat (4h30), Tangier (5h). CTM bus from Agadir (3h), Fez (8h), Ouarzazate (4h).

## Getting around Marrakech

**On foot**: The historic centre (medina, souks, Jemaa el Fna) is visited on foot. Maximum distance across the medina: 1.5 km. Small alleys don't allow vehicles.

**Calèche (horse-drawn carriage)**: Classic transport for journeys between the centre and Gueliz (modern district). Negotiate price before boarding. Typically 50-80 MAD per journey.

**Small taxi**: For journeys outside the medina. Insist on the meter or agree on a price first. Typical fare: 20-30 MAD within the city.

**Uber/Careem**: Available and more predictable in price than taxis.

## Where to stay

**Inside the medina — riad**: The most authentic experience. A riad is a traditional house built around an interior courtyard. The best ones offer complete silence 50 metres from the souk noise. Price range: £40-300/night depending on category. Those offering breakfast on the terrace with views to the Koutoubia (or the Atlas on clear days) are a category apart.

**Gueliz (new city)**: International chain hotels, more standardised. More practical for families with a car. Less immersive in the medina experience.

**Luxury riads in the Palmeraie**: Outside the centre, in a quiet area. Perfect as a resort, but you lose the ability to step out for an early morning walk through the medina before the souks open.

## Where to eat

The best tagines in Marrakech aren't at Jemaa el Fna's perimeter restaurants (expensive, inconsistent quality). They're in restaurants inside the medina, recognisable by a daily menu on a chalkboard, no theatrics, and a majority of local diners.

Best spots:
- **Rahba Kedima spice market**: Simple food stalls, good harira
- **Derb Djedid restaurants** (a street parallel to the souks): Excellent value
- **Riad restaurants** (open to non-guests): Complete Moroccan experience in a courtyard setting

## Practical tips

**Currency**: Moroccan Dirham (MAD / DH). Approximate rate: £1 = 14-15 MAD. Withdraw from local bank ATMs (BMC, Attijariwafa, BMCE) rather than airport exchange counters.

**Souk negotiation**: Opening price = 3× the real price. Negotiating is part of the ritual, not an aggression. Starting at half the asking price is a normal opener.

**Unofficial "guides"**: Men who spontaneously offer "help" navigating to the medina or souks earn commission in the shops they take you to. Decline politely and keep walking.

**Dress**: The medina is a conservative space. Covered shoulders and knees is appropriate — not strictly required but clearly the right call for comfort and respect.

## Marrakech as a base for the desert

Marrakech is the most common starting point for the [Sahara desert route](/en/blog/marrakech-complete-guide). From the city, the natural route climbs the High Atlas via the Tichka Pass (2,260m), descends to Ouarzazate, passes through Aït Ben Haddou and reaches Merzouga in 8-9 hours.
