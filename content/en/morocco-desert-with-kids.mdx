The Moroccan desert is an excellent family destination — more so than most people expect. Kids who can handle the long drive (the main challenge) tend to find the experience genuinely thrilling: a camel ride, sleeping in a tent under a sky full of stars, climbing a sand dune, watching the sunrise. These are experiences that stay. Here's how to make it work.

## The right age

There's no strict minimum, but practically:

**Under 3**: The desert is hard for very young children. Heat regulation is less efficient, the long driving days are exhausting, and basic camp bathroom situations can be difficult. Not impossible, but demanding for everyone.

**3–6 years**: Manageable with careful planning. Choose the easiest travel option (private car or tour), a mid-range camp with decent bathroom facilities, and avoid summer. Keep outdoor exposure limited to early morning and late afternoon. Kids this age often love the camel ride and the sand more than anything else.

**7 and above**: The sweet spot. Sandboarding is a hit, the camel ride is exciting rather than scary, kids can walk part of the dune trail, and they're old enough to appreciate the landscape. This age group often reports the desert as their favourite travel memory.

**Teenagers**: Generally excellent. Old enough to do everything (quad, 4x4 excursion, night walks), and the desert is unusual enough to cut through phone distraction — at least for a day.

---

## Season matters more with kids

**Spring (March–May) and autumn (October–November)**: The right choice for families. Temperatures are comfortable for children, and outdoor activities work at normal hours.

**Summer (June–August)**: Not recommended with young children. The heat (40–48 °C) is genuinely dangerous for children, who overheat faster than adults and don't always recognise their own heat stress. If summer is the only option, stay in the car between 10am and 5pm, ensure constant hydration, and limit outdoor activity strictly.

**Winter (December–February)**: Cold nights (below 0 °C in January) need proper equipment (warm sleeping bags for children, extra layers). Daytime is fine. If you plan to sleep in a tent, check that your children's sleeping gear is rated for the temperature.

## Transport with kids

**Private car or private tour**: Strongly recommended over bus travel with children. The road is 8–9 hours — on a bus, this is miserable. In a private car you can stop when needed, spread the journey over two days, and adjust pace to the family.

**Splitting the drive**: With children, two days southbound (overnight in Ouarzazate) and two days northbound (overnight somewhere on the return) turns a brutal driving marathon into a manageable journey with genuine sightseeing at Aït Ben Haddou and the gorges.

## Accommodation tips for families

**Camp selection**: Choose a mid-range or luxury camp, not a basic one. The main difference that matters for families: bathroom quality. A squat toilet at 2am in the dark, scrambling around in a shared area, is manageable for adults but stressful for young children. Private or semi-private bathroom facilities make the night infinitely more relaxed.

**Check for family rooms or adjacent tents**: Some camps offer family-sized haimas or two connected tents — worth asking about if you have young children who may be unsettled in an unfamiliar environment.

**Location**: Ask specifically how far the camp is from the road. For the desert magic to work, you need to be in the dunes, not on the edge of the village with car sounds and streetlights.

## Activities for kids

**Camel ride** (all ages, 4+): The safest and most universally loved activity. Children typically ride in front of an adult or are led on a separate, calmer camel by a guide. The 45–60 minute sunset trek is the perfect length for most children — long enough to be a proper experience, short enough not to become uncomfortable.

**Sandboarding** (6+ years): A wooden plank and a steep dune. Immediate hit with most kids who have any experience with sledging. Adults enjoy it too.

**Dune climbing** (any age): Simply walking up a large dune and running down is a complete activity in itself. The sand gives way with each step, the climb is tiring, and the slide down is instant gratification.

**4x4 excursion** (7+ years): An hour or two exploring the oases, salt lakes, and hamada around Merzouga. Better for older children who enjoy the landscape.

**Stargazing**: A pure experience that doesn't require preparation. Walk 10 minutes from camp on a clear night and lie down in the sand. The Milky Way overhead, total silence, no screens — most kids under 12 find this genuinely astonishing.

## Packing extras for kids

Beyond the [standard packing list](/en/blog/what-to-pack-sahara-desert):

- **Sun hat with neck protection** (essential for children; scalp burns fast)
- **Children's SPF 50+ sunscreen** — more than you think you'll need
- **Wet wipes** (water is limited at camps; wet wipes handle a lot of situations)
- **Children's rehydration sachets** (oral rehydration salts — quicker than water if a child becomes overheated)
- **Small headtorch/torch for each child** (for the 5am sunrise walk and any night trips to bathroom)
- **Familiar snacks**: the transition from Moroccan camp food to local taste can be abrupt for young children — having a few familiar snacks takes the pressure off meals

## Managing expectations

The long driving days are the main challenge. Break them up with proper stops (Aït Ben Haddou is ideal for kids — they can run around, climb, and explore) and carry car entertainment. The drives are worth it; the destination genuinely delivers for families who manage the journey.

The desert camp night is almost universally loved by children who experience it. Even children who are initially nervous about sleeping in a tent tend to report it as the highlight of the trip.
