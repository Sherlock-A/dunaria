Essaouira's medina looks like no other in Morocco. Its wider streets than Fès or Marrakech, its white limestone ramparts facing the Atlantic, and the contrast between European geometry and Amazigh decorative details make it a unique case in Moroccan historic architecture.

## History: the city a European architect designed

Essaouira has ancient roots — the Phoenicians, the Portuguese and the Berbers all left their mark on this strategic coastal point — but the city that exists today was built between **1764 and 1770** on the orders of Sultan **Sidi Mohammed ben Abdallah**.

The sultan wanted a modern trading port connecting Morocco to Europe. To design it he hired **Théodore Cornut**, a French military engineer, who laid out an urban plan with the logic of 18th-century European port cities: streets parallel to the sea, articulating squares, and defensive ramparts with bastions.

The result was a hybrid city: the urban layout is Western, the architecture of the houses is Moroccan, and the materials are local (local limestone, thuya wood, Amazigh ceramics).

In **2001, UNESCO listed Essaouira's medina** as an exceptional example of 18th-century colonial urbanism in Morocco.

## The ramparts: types and architecture

### Skala de la Ville

This is the **north bastion**, the most photogenic. Built to defend the city from maritime attacks, it has a battery of **26 bronze cannons** lined up facing the Atlantic. The cannons are from the 18th century and carry inscriptions in Spanish, Portuguese and Dutch — reflecting the European arms suppliers of the era.

The platform on the ramparts offers the best views of the city and ocean. Free access from Rue Skala, at the northern end of the medina.

### Skala du Port

This is the **south bastion**, next to the port. Less visited than the Skala de la Ville but equally interesting: historical cannons, views over the fishing port and lighthouse, and the calmer atmosphere of the two.

Access from outside the medina, next to the port entrance. Entry ~10 MAD.

### The perimeter ramparts

The rest of the wall surrounds the complete medina. The best way to understand the scale of the defences is to walk the exterior perimeter — it takes about 30-40 minutes.

## Inside the medina: areas and main streets

### The commercial axis

**Avenue de l'Istiqlal** and **Rue Mohammed el Qory** are the two main arteries. Shops, craft workshops and restaurants are concentrated here. These streets are wider than usual in Moroccan medinas — wide enough for a loaded donkey to pass on each side, which was the original criterion.

### The craftsmen's district

North of the medina, between the ramparts and the main square, is the workshop district. **Thuya** craftsmen (the local aromatic wood) have worked here for generations. You can see the complete process: cutting, drying, turning, polishing and lacquering.

### The Mellah and Jewish quarter

In the northwest of the medina, the **Jewish quarter** has different architecture: wooden balconies, some facades with Hebrew symbols, and the Slat Lkahal synagogue (restored, visits possible). Jewish presence in Essaouira was very significant until the mid-20th century — in some 19th-century years, Jews were 50% of the city's population.

### Plaza Moulay Hassan

The central public space of the medina. Terrace cafés, street musicians, merchants and tourists mix in a genuine atmosphere. It's not staged for tourists — it's the real life of the city.

## How to visit the medina

**On foot, without a guide**: perfectly possible. The medina is compact (you can cross it in 15 minutes) and more legible than Fès. Main streets are signposted. There's no labyrinth to absorb you.

**With a local guide**: recommended if you want detailed historical context. Official guides can be hired at the Tourist Office (Bab Marrakech). A 1-2 hour guide costs 150-250 MAD.

**Best time**: heat isn't a problem in Essaouira (the sea breeze compensates), so you can go any time. But to photograph the north ramparts, early morning light (8am-10am) is best. For the square atmosphere, sunset (5pm-8pm).

## Medina architecture: details to notice

- **Carved wooden doors**: many houses retain their original cedar doors decorated with Islamic geometry.
- **Mashrabiya lattice windows**: wooden screens that filter light and protect interior privacy.
- **Stucco plasterwork**: in the oldest houses, interior walls have carved plaster in geometric patterns.
- **Zellige tiles**: in thresholds, fountains and commercial halls.
- **Limestone**: unlike other southern cities (which use red earth), Essaouira uses local limestone that gives the city its dominant white colour.

## The medina at night

Essaouira doesn't have Marrakech's nightlife, but the medina at night has its own charm: craftsmen closing their workshops, the scent of thuya wood in the air, oil lamps in some alleyways, musicians on the square.

If you're staying overnight, walk the medina between 9pm and 11pm when the mass tourists have returned to their hotels.
